Tips for Financial Cybersecurity

Enable Multifactor Authentication

Multifactor authentication (MFA) adds a critical second layer of security beyond your password. Use an authenticator app or biometrics like a fingerprint rather than SMS codes when possible, since SIM-swapping attacks can bypass text message verification. MFA significantly reduces the likelihood of a criminal taking over your account even if your password is stolen.

Watch What You Click

Phishing emails and smishing texts remain the most common entry points for account takeovers. Never click links or download attachments from unsolicited messages claiming to be from your bank. Instead, type the bank's official URL directly into your browser or use its mobile app. Cybercriminals also create fake websites that mimic financial institution login pages to steal your credentials.

Use Strong, Unique Passwords

Use a different password for every financial account and change them regularly. A password manager can generate and store complex passwords that mix uppercase and lowercase letters, numbers, and special characters. Avoid using personal information like your name or birthdate, and never share passwords over email, text, or phone.

Monitor Accounts and Statements

Review your account activity and monthly statements as soon as they are available. Set up text or email alerts for large withdrawals, address changes, and online transactions. Watch for warning signs such as unfamiliar transactions, missing statements, unexpected alerts about account changes, or a sudden loss of mobile phone service, which could indicate a SIM-swap attack.

Secure Your Devices and Networks

Keep your operating system, browser, and security software set to update automatically. Avoid using public Wi-Fi for banking — if you must, use a trusted VPN. At home, secure your network with strong encryption and a robust password. Only enter financial information on websites that display "https" and a padlock icon in the address bar on every page, not just the login screen.

Treat Your Personal Information Like Cash

Your Social Security number, account numbers, and credit card details are valuable targets. Only provide them when you are certain of the recipient's legitimacy. Shred physical documents containing financial information before discarding them. If your credentials are ever compromised in a data breach, change the password immediately on that account and any other account that used the same login information.
